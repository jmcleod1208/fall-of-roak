# Overview

> The top-level pitch for your game and world. Keep this tight — if it gets long, move details to other files.

## Elevator Pitch
<!-- 1-2 sentences. What is the game, what's the hook? -->

A story-driven auto-battler RPG where four heroes rally their village and march against Ro'ak, a half-orc warlord whose corrupting army threatens to swallow the world — but a deeper, older shadow stirs behind him, and the war they think they're fighting is only the beginning.

**(Internal — full twist):** Ro'ak is not the true enemy. He is the vessel of Noctheris, an old god using him to corrupt the world from within. Defeating Ro'ak is only the threshold of the real war.

## Logline
<!-- One sentence describing the core story conflict. "A [protagonist] must [goal] before [stakes]." -->

The last four survivors of a village razed by the warlord Ro'ak must march across Eadenreach and cut him down before the corruption he spreads devours every kingdom still standing.

## Tone & Vibe
<!-- Grimdark? High fantasy? Whimsical? Melancholy? List 3-5 adjectives. -->

**Grounded sword-and-sorcery** — Witcher-adjacent. A lived-in medieval world where magic is rare enough to feel wondrous and dangerous, and where dragons and old gods are half-believed fables until they aren't.

**Adjectives:** grounded, mythic, hopeful, intimate, dangerous

**Texture:**
- **Heavy moments** — the weight of a razed home, the cost of war, the slow creep of corruption
- **Bright moments** — a father teaching his son to swing a blade, a wife and husband sharing a quiet fire, an old friend cracking a joke when everyone needs it most
- **Mythic moments** — a dragon glimpsed at the horizon, a whisper from something older than the world, a relic that shouldn't exist

The four-person party is a **family unit** — father, mother, son, and the father's lifelong friend (uncle figure / mentor to the boy). This intimacy is the emotional anchor under the epic stakes.

## Themes
<!-- What is the story actually ABOUT under the surface? (e.g., loss, redemption, the cost of power) -->

- **Loss and the courage to keep going** — The party's lives were shattered before the story began. Everything they do is shadowed by what they've already lost. The question isn't whether grief disappears, but whether you can carry it and still walk forward.
- **Family bonds as armor** — The four heroes are a family in fact and in friendship. Their love for one another is what keeps the corruption from reaching them. In a world where Ro'ak's army breaks people apart, the party's strength is that they refuse to break.
- **Bringing evil to justice** — Some wounds can't be healed, only answered. The march on Ro'ak is not just a war; it's a reckoning. The story honors the moral weight of that — justice is earned, not given.
- **Rebuilding what was taken** — The village mechanic is the theme made playable. Stone by stone, neighbor by neighbor, the players reclaim what was burned. Loss is the beginning, not the end.

**Intended emotional arc:** The player begins in grief and ends in **joy and relief** — not a hollow triumph, but the earned peace of people who lost everything, fought through it together, and built something new on the other side.

## Inspirations
<!-- Games, books, films, shows that capture the feel you want. Note WHAT specifically you're borrowing from each. -->

### Tone & Writing
- **The Witcher 3** — Grounded sword-and-sorcery. Magic that feels rare and dangerous. Quiet conversations between hardened people that hit harder than the swordfights, broken up by rare moments of lightheartedness.

### Combat & Party Design
- **Battle Chasers: Nightwar** — Turn-based party combat with strong character identity and clear role distinction. **Key difference:** our combat auto-resolves. Player success comes from how they build, equip, and arrange the party *before* the battle — not moment-to-moment input.

### Village & Settlement
- *(No specific inspiration — designing from scratch.)* The player rebuilds the razed village into a thriving city. Settlement progression grants combat and resource buffs, tying the rebuilding loop directly into the war effort.

### World & Setting
- **Lord of the Rings** — Classic high-fantasy framework: humans, elves, dwarves, orcs, etc. A world with deep history and many peoples.
- **Game of Thrones** — Grounded combat. Magic is rare and unsettling when it appears. Kingdoms ruled by kings and queens with real political weight; the world feels lived-in and consequential.

### The Old Gods & The Twist
- **Lovecraft** — The maddening effect of contact with the old gods. Ro'ak isn't hollowed out — he's *filled*. He's been Noctheris's vessel since before he was born, and her power has stoked his rage and ambition into something monstrous, granting him terrible physical strength and corrupting magical reach. He knows she is with him, serves her willingly, and intends to dominate the world in her name.
- **World of Warcraft's Old Gods** — Physically present but hidden beneath the world. Not distant cosmic abstractions — **buried**, sleeping, exerting influence outward. Noctheris is somewhere on this world, not in another dimension.

## Player Fantasy
<!-- What does the player get to feel/do that they can't in real life? -->

**The avenging family.** *"Something monstrous took our home. I march with the people I love, each of us carrying our own reason to see it ended — and together, we will."*

The player doesn't inhabit a single hero. They live inside **all four party members** in turn — feeling each one's grief, each one's resolve, each one's reason to see Ro'ak fall. The party is bound by shared loss, but every blade they raise is sharpened by a private wound.

**Key emotional moments the game must deliver:**

- **First battle** — *exciting but humble.* The party is not yet legendary. They are survivors who have decided to fight back. Combat should feel earned and hard-won, not power-fantasy clean.
- **First rebuilt building** — *hopeful and proud.* The first stone laid on the ashes of the old village. A small thing that signals: we are not just surviving, we are *building*. The player should feel a quiet swell of dignity here.
- **The Noctheris reveal** — *dread and shock.* The moment the player realizes the war they thought they were fighting was a smaller war inside a larger, older one. The ground under the story shifts. What they thought was the end is the threshold.
- **Ro'ak's death** — *celebration and relief.* The years of marching, the years of grief, the friends and family lost along the way — all of it answered. A moment for the party (and the player) to finally exhale before whatever comes next.

## Core Pillars
<!-- 3-5 non-negotiable design principles that every feature/story decision should support. -->

1. **Family is the heart.** Every story beat, every battle, every village upgrade should reinforce the bond between the four heroes. If a feature doesn't serve that bond, cut it.

2. **Strategy lives in preparation, not in the fight.** Combat auto-resolves. The player's agency comes from how they build the party — equipment, formation, skills, synergies. The battle is the test of the plan, not the place where you make the plan.

3. **Magic is rare, and it costs.** Magic should never feel routine. Every spell, every relic, every glimpse of the supernatural should feel like a door opening to something larger than the player.

4. **Loss is real. Hope is earned by exacting justice upon evil.** The story doesn't shy from grief, and it doesn't grant peace cheaply. Every dark moment is answered by the party's refusal to let evil go unanswered.

5. **The village is the story made playable.** Rebuilding isn't a side mode. It's a thematic mirror of the main quest — every stone laid is a refusal to let Ro'ak's fire be the last word.

## Out of Scope
<!-- Things this game is intentionally NOT. Helps keep scope tight. -->

### Features we are NOT building
- Open-world exploration
- Real-time / action combat
- Voice acting
- Multiplayer / co-op
- Romance options
- Character creation / custom protagonists
- Branching story / multiple endings (single linear story, single ending)
- Procedural content / roguelike runs
- 3D graphics (2D only)
- Permadeath / player-character death

### Tonal lines we will not cross
- Sexual content
- Graphic torture or extreme gore *(some gore is acceptable when it serves the moment)*
- Modern political commentary
- Comedy as the dominant register *(humor exists, but never breaks tone)*

### Undecided / to revisit
- **Crafting system** — possibly included, but only if it ties directly into the village rebuilding loop. Standalone crafting (gear-from-materials menus disconnected from settlement progression) is out.
