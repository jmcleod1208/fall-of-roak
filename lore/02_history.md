# History & Timeline

> The past that shapes the present. Start with a bare timeline, expand events as needed.

## Eras

The history of Halig is divided into five great ages. The earlier ones are mythic and uncertain; the later ones are remembered in song, record, and living memory. The five eras are:

### 1. The Making
The Creator made Halig, the cosmos, and all life within it. He shaped the Old Gods first, granting each a sacred purpose to tend and protect the world. Mortals — humans, elves, dwarves, orcs — came after, made to live, believe, and join him in the Life After.

*The Making is **not known with certainty**.* No mortal alive remembers it. No record of it survives. **The creation story is a core tenet of faith in the Creator** — believed across every people and culture — but it is faith, not proof. What scholars and seekers know of the Old Gods' original purposes (and what we as authors know of the truth) was preserved in fragments, songs, and forbidden lore.

### 2. The Turning
The Old Gods fell, one by one, to the very desires they were meant to keep in check. Noctheris to her shame, Visstheri to his greed, Aqueloth to her hunger, Drakhalion to his fire, Morhaem to the worship he was meant to channel. Each turned from the Creator. Each was punished or withdrew into the dark.

This era ends the age of divine guardianship. The world's protectors had become its predators. From this point on, **mortals stand alone** — with only faith, and the rare and dangerous gift of magic, between them and what their guardians became.

### 3. The Age of Crowns
The long age of mortal kingdoms. Humans rose to political dominance, elves kept to the Old Forest and built Elenathir, dwarves dug deep into the mountains around Dreadvik, and orc tribes spread across the wilder lands of Halig under their own codes of honor.

**Crownhaven was founded** during this era and became the seat of a unified human kingdom across Eadenreach. This is the longest and most lived-in age — the era of dynasties, trade, scholarship, slow building, and ordinary peace. Most of "history as people know it" happened here.

### 4. The Hollowing
The dwarves stripped the mountains around Dreadvik of their riches and emigrated en masse to the far north. Their forge-cities were abandoned. The mountain passes fell silent. The dwarves never sent word back — not a letter, not a trader, not an envoy.

Dreadvik became empty, forgotten, and slowly mythologized: the cold east, the place of legend, the ground where mothers told their children not to wander. **The Hollowing made Dreadvik ready** — unguarded, unwatched — for what would come next.

### 5. The Present
The current era. Ro'ak has risen in Dreadvik. His army moves west. The village of the four heroes has burned. **Crownhaven has just fallen.** The kingdom is reeling, leaderless. The corruption is spreading from the east.

What in-world chroniclers will call this era is not yet decided — they may not even survive to name it. The story of this game is the story of the Present.

## Timeline

*Dating system: **AC** (After Crowns) and **BC** (Before Crowns), anchored to the founding of Crownhaven (Year 0). The Present is **Year 500 AC**.*

### Pre-History
- **The Making.** Beyond recorded time. The Creator made Halig, the Old Gods, and life. Unknowable in detail; held in faith.
- **The Turning.** Beyond recorded time. The Old Gods fell. The age of divine guardianship ended.

### The Age of Crowns and the Hollowing

- **~1,000 BC** — **The Hollowing.** The dwarves strip the mountains around Dreadvik of their riches and emigrate to the far north. Their forge-cities are abandoned. They fall silent and have not been heard from since. Dreadvik becomes empty — and would remain empty for a thousand years.

- **Year 0 AC** — **The Founding of Crownhaven.** A unified human kingdom is established across Eadenreach. The calendar of the present age begins.

- **Year 250 AC** — **Ro'ak is born** to a human mother and an orc father. Noctheris has claimed him in the womb; he is her vessel from his first breath. *(Full backstory to be detailed in `04_characters.md`.)*

- **Years 250–499 AC** — **The Hidden Centuries.** Ro'ak dwells beneath Dreadvik with Noctheris, gaining power under her whispers. He does not march, does not raid, does not announce himself. To the kingdoms of the west, these are ordinary centuries — kings rule, harvests come and go, the world breathes easy. The danger growing in the east is invisible.

- **Year 400 AC** — **The village of the four heroes is founded** in a green valley of the Greenfields. Three or four generations of villagers will live, die, and be buried there before its end. Modest, beloved, ordinary.

### The Present

- **Year 499 AC** — **Ro'ak emerges from Dreadvik.** After 250 years beneath the mountain, he leads his army openly for the first time. The corruption begins to spread. The first burned villages, the first battles in the east. Word reaches Crownhaven, but the kingdom underestimates the threat.

- **Spring, Year 500 AC** — **The village burns.** One full year after Ro'ak emerged, his army reaches the heart of the Greenfields. The four heroes' home is razed. The story begins.

- **Spring–Summer, Year 500 AC** — The party marches east through the Greenfields and into the Old Forest. The elves of Elenathir warn them of corruption in the woods.

- **Days before reaching Demesne, Year 500 AC** — **Crownhaven falls.** Ro'ak's army razes the capital. The kingdom is broken. Survivors scatter into the mist of the moors.

- **The party arrives in Demesne, Year 500 AC** — fires still burning, smoke still rising. They find no one to help them.

- **Late Year 500 AC, into Winter** — **The party reaches Dreadvik.** The final march toward Ro'ak's fortress begins.

## Major Events (Detailed)

*Three historical events get full detail here. Present-day events (the village burning, Crownhaven falling, Ro'ak's birth) live in `09_plot.md` and `04_characters.md`.*

---

### The Turning

- **When:** Pre-history. Beyond recorded time.
- **What happened:** The five Old Gods fell from their sacred purposes — not all at once, but **one by one** across an unknowable span of time. Some fell before mortals were even made; others fell only after meeting them, corrupted by what they were supposed to keep in check. Each fell to their own desire: Noctheris to her shame, Visstheri to greed, Aqueloth to hunger, Drakhalion to fire, Morhaem to the worship he was meant to channel toward the Creator.
- **The Creator's response:** A mix of punishment and withdrawal. Some Old Gods were openly punished — Visstheri was condemned to walk alone in the dark places of cities, unseen and unsated. Others were not struck down so much as **left to fall further**, the Creator simply withdrawing from them and ceasing to acknowledge them.
- **Key figures:** The Creator. The five Old Gods (Noctheris, Visstheri, Aqueloth, Drakhalion, Morhaem).
- **How it's remembered:** **The Turning is in the songs and ghost stories.** Children of every culture hear of it before they hear of any king. The names of the Old Gods come down through lullabies, hearth-tales, drinking songs, and the warnings mothers give before bed. Most who sing them think they are myths. Some know better.
- **Consequences still felt today:** Every danger in the present world ultimately traces back to the Turning. The world's appointed guardians became its hidden predators — and mortal kind has been alone with them ever since. Ro'ak, Noctheris's vessel, is the most active expression of the Turning's long shadow.

---

### The Hollowing

- **When:** ~1,000 BC.
- **What happened:** The dwarves of Eadenreach **dug too deep**. In their relentless mining of the mountains around Dreadvik, they breached something they should never have touched: **a crack in the prison of Noctheris.** They felt her stirring beneath them — felt something *looking back through the dark* — and they panicked.

  They did not warn the other peoples of Halig. They did not seal what they had broken. They simply **packed and left, quietly and in haste**, abandoning forge-cities, tools, machines, and treasures too heavy to carry. Within a single generation, the entire dwarven population of Eadenreach was gone north — silent ever since.

- **Key figures:** Unknown. The names of the dwarves who first felt Noctheris through the crack are lost to history (or kept among the dwarves in the far north, if any of them still live).
- **What was left behind:** Empty forge-cities riddling the Dreadvik mountains. Abandoned tools, dormant machines, sealed vaults of treasure too cumbersome to flee with. **And the crack itself — unsealed, ungarded, deepening.** It is from this crack that Noctheris reaches out into the world. It is somewhere beneath what is now Ro'ak's fortress.
- **Faith:** The dwarves once had their own way of worshipping the Creator, but their tradition has been **lost to time**. Most dwarves today — the few that remain in Eadenreach — do not worship at all.
- **How it's remembered:** As a strange, almost embarrassing event in mortal history. *"The dwarves left."* No one knows why. The few dwarves who stayed behind in Eadenreach either don't know themselves or refuse to say.
- **Consequences still felt today:** Dreadvik became empty and forgotten — unwatched, unguarded, mythologized. **It became the perfect ground for Noctheris to grow her vessel.** Every step of Ro'ak's rise traces back to the Hollowing. The dwarves did not start the corruption — but they unsealed the door it would walk through.

---

### The Founding of Crownhaven

- **When:** Year 0 AC.
- **What happened:** **King Alaric and Queen Seraphina Valerian** founded Crownhaven and unified the scattered human peoples of Eadenreach under a single crown and a single faith. The kingdom was established explicitly as **a unified human realm under the Creator's faith** — not a theocracy, but a kingdom whose ruling house openly held the Creator at the center of its public life.
- **Key figures:** King Alaric Valerian. Queen Seraphina Valerian. *(Founders of House Valerian, which has held the throne for 500 years — right up until Crownhaven's recent fall.)*
- **The kingdom's character:** Unified, lawful, faith-anchored. Eadenreach's roads, its records, its currency, its calendar all flow from Crownhaven. The kingdom did not spread by conquest so much as by being the obvious center.
- **How it's remembered:** With pride and celebration. **All human settlements in Eadenreach hold an annual Founding Festival** marking the day Alaric and Seraphina took the throne. Bonfires, music, a shared meal, often a brief sermon or remembrance from a village priest. It's the closest thing to a national holiday in a kingdom without much pomp.
- **Consequences still felt today:** Crownhaven has just fallen. Its 500-year throne is empty, its capital is rubble, **and the Founding Festival of Year 500 AC will be the last one most living humans ever celebrated** — it took place only weeks before the village burned. The party may yet remember dancing at it. The pain of that memory is one of the quiet weights they carry east.

## Myths & Legends

*A note on truth: in Halig, **myths and legends are generally true**. The stories told around fires and sung in taverns are not lies dressed as folklore — they are folklore wrapping real history, real beings, and real places. Players who listen to NPCs gain actual knowledge of the world.*

---

### Songs of the Old Gods

Five songs, one for each Old God, sung across Halig in different forms. Most who sing them think they are nursery rhymes or hearth-songs. They are not.

**Lullaby of the Wanting Mother** *(sung to fussy children at bedtime, in many regions — nobody remembers who wrote it)*
> *Hush little one, hush little one,*
> *Down where the deep things weep,*
> *A mother is waiting, a mother is wanting,*
> *Hush, little one, sleep.*

**The Thief in the Lane** *(a city-folk warning rhyme, often sung by parents to children walking home at dusk)*
> *Mind your purse and mind your gate,*
> *Mind the alleys when it's late.*
> *Coin or jewel or paper note —*
> *He'll have them all and have your coat.*

**The Hullbreaker's Song** *(a sailor's drinking chant in coastal taverns; sung loud and badly so as not to be overheard by the sea)*
> *Don't sail east of the Black Quill light,*
> *Don't sail south of the Hooded Head.*
> *She who hungers in the night*
> *Counts our hulls among her bread.*

**The First Flame** *(a hearth-song sung by farmers when wildfires move through distant lands; older than anyone alive)*
> *When the woods burn far away,*
> *And the wind smells thick of pine,*
> *Bow your head and softly say,*
> *"It is not me, it is not mine."*

**The Red Cup** *(an old verse that has fallen out of fashion in cities and is not sung in polite company)*
> *Don't kneel to the cup that drinks of you,*
> *Don't bleed for a god of bone.*
> *The chalice that swallows your faith and you*
> *Is hollow, and lives alone.*

These songs preserve the names and natures of the Old Gods through generations. Most who sing them are doing simple folk-singing. **Every word, however, is true.** A child who is afraid of the lullaby is afraid of something real.

---

### The Founders' Vision

The legend most often told of **King Alaric and Queen Seraphina Valerian** is not of battle, not of conquest, not of dragon-slaying. It is of a **shared vision** — the moment they were called to build the kingdom.

The story holds that Alaric and Seraphina, before they were married or crowned, lived in different parts of fragmented Eadenreach. Each, in their separate lives, dreamed the same dream on the same night: **a single hearth-fire burning in a great hall, and around it every people of the human realm, eating from one table.** Each woke convinced the dream was a calling from the Creator. Each set out to find the other, not knowing the other existed.

They met. They married. They built the hall. They called the peoples in. **And the kingdom that became Crownhaven was founded around a hearth-fire that has been kept burning, in unbroken vigil, for 500 years.**

It was kept burning, that is, until the night Crownhaven fell.

The love between Alaric and Seraphina is the part of the legend most loved by ordinary people. They are remembered not as conquerors but as **two people who found each other through faith and built a kingdom on it.** Their story is told at every Founding Festival, often by the youngest priest in a village — a tradition meant to remind the next generation that great things can be built from small, faithful beginnings.

*(True. Every word.)*

---

### Local Legends of Fernfield

*The four heroes' village is called **Fernfield**, named for the wide ferny hollows that ring its valley. It sat in the Greenfields for 100 years before Ro'ak's army razed it. Two legends in particular were beloved by the villagers — the kind of stories grandparents told children at bedtime, and that lovers retold to each other under the village's old trees.*

#### The Sleeping Stag

In the gentle woods west of Fernfield, **a great white stag is said to wander.** He is enormous, antlers like a vault of bone, and he does not graze — he **sleeps.** He sleeps in clearings where the moonlight pools. He sleeps where the wind goes quiet. He sleeps where children get lost, and he wakes only when they come close, and he leads them home.

The villagers of Fernfield called him **the Sleeping Stag**. To see him was good fortune. To be guided by him was a blessing for life. The old women of the village swore they had seen him, once, in their youth.

*(True. The Sleeping Stag is a real creature — a forest spirit of the Old Forest's outermost reaches. The Greenfields woods are technically the same forest as the Old Forest, and the Stag has wandered the western edge for centuries. Whether he still walks now, with corruption creeping into the Old Forest, is unknown.)*

#### The Founder's Oak

At the center of Fernfield stood an enormous oak — older than the village itself, broad enough that ten children holding hands could not fully circle its trunk. The villagers called it **the Founder's Oak.**

The legend held that when Fernfield's first settlers came to the valley a hundred years ago, the oak was already there — ancient, alone in the meadow, its branches spread like a roof. The founders took it as a sign and built the village around it. **Every wedding in Fernfield was held beneath the oak. Every newborn was brought to it within their first week of life and laid against its bark for a moment, for blessing. Every villager who died had a leaf from the oak placed in their hand before burial.**

Lovers carved their initials into its lower trunk. Children dared each other to climb its highest branch. The oak was, in every meaningful sense, **the heart of Fernfield.**

*(True. The oak was real, and it was the village's beloved center. It burned with the rest of Fernfield. The party will pass its blackened stump on their way out of the valley, and none of them will speak of it.)*

---

### Sea-Tales of the Hullbreaker

In every port town along Eadenreach's coasts, sailors keep an unspoken set of rules about the deep waters. **Aqueloth is rarely named directly.** To name her is to draw her attention. Sailors call her **the Hullbreaker** when they must speak of her at all, and many use no name at all — just *she*, *her*, or *the deep*.

The sea-tales of the Hullbreaker are practical, not romantic. They are warnings, not adventures. The most common include:

- **"Don't sail past the Black Quill light at moonless tide."** A specific stretch of water along the eastern coast where ships have vanished without wreckage for centuries. The light still burns, kept by a lonely keeper who has the look of someone who has heard things from the water.

- **"Three dolphins ahead, you turn back."** Sailors believe that when dolphins flee a stretch of sea in a tight pod of three, the Hullbreaker is hunting beneath. *(True — the dolphins know.)*

- **"Never finish the Hullbreaker's song alone."** The drinking chant in coastal taverns is sung loud and badly by groups, but never finished by one person alone, and never sung at sea. To sing of her at sea is to invite her up.

- **"The captains' charts."** Almost every veteran captain in Eadenreach keeps a personal sea-chart with certain waters circled in red ink — places where ships have been lost. **There are no captains' charts that disagree on which waters are red.** Every captain marks the same coordinates. Aqueloth's hunting grounds have been the same for a thousand years.

No cult worships her. No shrine bears her name. **She is the Old God who is most respected, and least loved, in mortal memory.**

*(All true. Aqueloth has not been encountered directly in this game's story — the party marches inland — but coastal NPCs may share these tales, and they shape the player's understanding of what the Old Gods truly are.)*

## The Present Day

*Spring, Year 500 AC. The world the party wakes into the morning after Fernfield burns.*

---

### The Crown is gone

Crownhaven has fallen. **House Valerian has been wiped out entirely** — the line that ruled for 500 years is ash. There is no surviving heir to rally to, no banner to raise, no throne to defend. The kingdom of Eadenreach, in any meaningful political sense, **no longer exists.** What remains is a collection of orphaned towns, lordless lands, and frightened people.

### There is no organized resistance

No general has risen. No surviving knight-captain has taken the field. No high priest has called the faithful to arms. **Every town is on its own.** Some lords may eventually try to raise local levies, but as of the game's start, no coordinated defense is forming. The west is a body without a head.

### The elves have sealed Elenathir

The capital of the Old Forest has shut its gates. **Corruption is creeping through the outer woods**, and the elves have committed everything they have to holding the line around their city. **Travelers will not pass through Elenathir's gates in this story.** What the party will encounter in the Old Forest is **elven scouts, rangers, and warriors fighting a quiet war on the forest's edge** — exhausted, stretched thin, willing to warn and sometimes to help, but unable to spare strength for the human realms. The elves are losing ground slowly, and they know it.

### The Greenfields is rebuilding

The other villages of the Greenfields, west of Fernfield's ruin, are not panicking. They are not fleeing. **They are pulling together.** Word of Crownhaven's fall has reached them; they have grieved; and they have set their jaws and gone back to work. The Greenfields' people are **cohesive, neighborly, and stubborn** — the kind of folk who answer disaster with raised barns and shared bread. They will not let this break them. They will not let it ruin their home.

This is the soil out of which the four heroes grew, and it is why **rebuilding Fernfield over the course of the game is possible at all** — not because one party can do it alone, but because the Greenfields' people show up. Refugees arrive with what they could carry. Neighbor villages send timber, tools, hands. The village rises again, slowly, while the heroes march east. The home they are fighting for is being remade behind them.

### Ro'ak's army is not one army — it is a host

Refugees and scouts describe a force unlike anything in living memory. It includes:

- **Humans who have turned** — soldiers, brigands, the desperate, the corrupted.
- **Orcs from the eastern wilds** who have abandoned their honor codes and joined.
- **Elves who have fallen to corruption** — rare, and chilling to those who see them.
- **Wildlife beasts** twisted by Noctheris's reach: dire wolves, corrupted bears, blackened stags.
- **Creations of Noctheris herself**, made beneath Dreadvik in places no mortal has seen:
  - **Ogres, golems, witches**
  - **Skeletons, zombies, ghouls**
  - **Ghosts and wraiths**
  - **Vampires and liches**
  - **Elementals of fire, ice, and stone**
  - **Wyverns** — the first time most westerners have ever seen one.

This is why the kingdoms have no answer. **No army in the Age of Crowns was ever built to fight a host like this.** Knights trained for orc raiders meet a wraith and die. Castles built for siege weapons meet a wyvern and burn. Ro'ak does not have an army — he has **the unmaking of the world** at his back.

### Faith is splitting

The fall of Crownhaven — the kingdom built on the Founders' Vision, the kingdom whose hearth-fire burned 500 years — has cracked the spiritual life of common people in two.

- **Some are losing faith.** *"Where was the Creator when Crownhaven burned? Where is he now?"* Chapels stand empty. Old prayers go unsaid. A quiet despair is settling into homes that used to sing.
- **Some are gaining faith.** *"This is the time the Creator was warning us about. Now is when belief matters."* Other chapels are fuller than they have been in living memory. Strangers pray together. Old people teach young people the words.

The world is sorting itself, person by person, into those who will hold and those who will let go. **Noctheris feeds on the ones who let go.** Some of the strange new fringe sects rising in the chaos may not be what they appear.

### The Demesne is fog and danger

Between the Old Forest and Dreadvik lies the Demesne — the moors that hold the rubble of Crownhaven. **The mist that now rolls across these moors is new — and it is old.** Before Crownhaven, the Demesne was a haunted, fog-drowned waste; travelers vanished in it, and the old maps marked it as cursed ground. **When Alaric and Seraphina founded the kingdom on shared faith, the mist lifted.** For 500 years, the moors were clear — windswept, austere, but walkable. Caravans crossed them in daylight. Children grew up never knowing the fog their great-grandparents feared.

**Now Crownhaven is ash, and the mist has returned.** It rose the night the capital fell, as if it had been waiting. Older folk who remember the stories of their grandparents recognize it. They know what its return means.

Inside the mist, the Demesne is **two things at once:**

- **A landscape of broken refugees.** Survivors of Crownhaven's fall scatter across the moors — wounded soldiers, displaced families, hermits, half-mad priests, lone children. Most will not survive the year. Some will help the party. Some will need help the party cannot give.
- **Already contested ground.** Ro'ak's outriders sweep the moors, hunting survivors. Bandits prey on what the outriders miss. Desperate holdouts dig into ruins and fight whoever comes near. The mist hides all of them. **There is no safe road through the Demesne** — only choices about which danger to face next.

Crownhaven's smoke still rises when the party arrives, and the fog of the old world rises with it.
