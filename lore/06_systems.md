# World Systems — Halig

> The rules of how things work in Halig. Magic, faith, corruption, craft, and the underlying logic that holds the world together. **Everything in this world ultimately comes from the Creator.** Even the things that have been twisted away from him.

---

## Part 1 — The Creator (First Principle)

Before anything else, the rule that governs everything:

**The Creator made all things.** The land, the sea, the sky, the stars. The peoples — humans, elves, dwarves, orcs. The Old Gods themselves were once part of his work, before they turned. He is the **First and the Highest**, the source of all that exists.

He does not speak directly. He does not appear. He acts through **what already exists** — through the world, through the faithful, through the gifts he has given. **His reasons are always left up to interpretation.** This is intentional. Faith without certainty is the only kind of faith that means anything.

**He sees what is truly in a person's heart.** This is the rule that governs all magic in Halig. Words and rites and prayers are not enough. **He knows.** And he answers accordingly.

---

## Part 2 — The Magic System

### Source

**All magic comes from the Creator.** There is no other true source. Every form of magic in Halig is either:

- **A gift directly given by the Creator** to a worthy heart (faith magic, nature magic, creation magic), or
- **A gift twisted away from him** by an Old God who exploits a personal weakness in the receiver (corrupted magic).

Even Noctheris's power, in the end, is **stolen Creator-gift, perverted through exploitation.** She cannot make magic from nothing. She can only take what is already there and bend it.

### The Two Requirements

To wield magic, a person needs both:

1. **Belief** — sincere, devout faith in the Creator. *He knows what is truly in your heart.* An evil person cannot fake their way into holy fire. A doubting person cannot accidentally stumble into it. Belief must be real.
2. **Aptitude** — a random gift, given by the Creator to whomever he chooses. **Nobody understands why he chooses who he chooses.** A devout person without aptitude prays, lives faithfully, and never channels a single flame. A devout person *with* aptitude can. *His reasons are always left up to interpretation.*

> Vallen Skye is devout. Whether the Creator has given him aptitude is unknown. He could become a paladin through years of study and faith — **but only if the Creator decides to answer him.**

### What Magic Can Do

- Heal wounds (slowly, with prayer and herb-craft; or quickly, with true gift).
- Summon holy fire that burns the unfaithful but warms the faithful.
- Call wards and shields against corruption.
- Speak with the spirits of the land (elven tradition).
- Craft objects that hold and channel power (dwarven tradition).
- Smite — direct strike of Creator's judgment, devastating but always at a personal cost.

### What Magic Cannot Do

- **Resurrect the dead.** When someone dies, they are with the Creator. Calling them back is a violation of his will and the work of the Old Gods only.
- **Force another person's heart.** Magic cannot make someone love, fear, or believe against their will. (Noctheris *exploits* a weakness — she does not create one.)
- **Create from nothing.** The Creator created. His servants imitate, channel, and shape, but they do not *originate.*
- **Be wielded by the unfaithful.** Period.
- **Be wielded by the faithful without aptitude.** No matter how hard they try.

### Cost & Risk

- **Faith magic is exhausting.** A paladin who burns too bright too long collapses. Van's holy fire has a daily ceiling. He prays before sleep to refill the well.
- **Smiting carries the deepest cost.** A true smite — direct invocation of the Creator's judgment — leaves the caster prostrate for hours, sometimes days. *Use it only when it must be used.*
- **Healing draws on the healer's own life-force when the wound is grave.** Calypsa can mend a sprain without consequence. Mending a mortal wound costs her sleep, blood, and sometimes years.
- **Corrupted magic costs the bearer their soul, one piece at a time.** They do not feel it leaving until it is mostly gone.

### Public Perception

- Magic is **rare, respected, and quietly feared.** A village may know one healer, one wise woman, maybe one wandering priest with the gift. They are not common.
- Faith magic worn openly (Van's burning gauntlets) is **a sign of clear devotion** and draws respect from Creator-faithful and unease from everyone else.
- **Corrupted magic is heresy.** Anyone seen wielding it openly will be hunted. The Mothers and the lieutenants do their work in shadow for a reason.

## Part 3 — The Schools of Creator Magic

All of these are the same magic, expressed through different traditions of worship. **The Creator does not care which tradition you follow.** He cares what is in your heart.

### Faith Magic (Human Tradition — Van's path)

- **Worship:** Direct prayer to the Creator. Chapels, hymns, scripture, sacrament.
- **Manifestation:** **Holy fire.** Holy shield. Smiting. Healing through laying-on of hands. Wards against corruption.
- **Visual signature:** **Golden flame.** Warm, bright, alive. Comforting to the faithful, painful to the corrupted.
- **Practitioners:** Paladins (warriors of faith), priests, lay healers. Found across the Greenfields, the Demesne (formerly), and most human regions.
- **Van's expression:** Holy fire from his gauntlets, holy shield from his bracer, smiting blows from his mace. The chapel where his parents died was where he first felt the gift answer him.

### Nature Magic (Elven Tradition)

- **Worship:** The elves worship the **Creator through his creation.** They tend the land, the trees, the rivers, the beasts; they believe this is the highest worship — to honor the work by caring for it. **They acknowledge the Creator is most holy and most high.** They understand humans worship the same Creator in a different way, and they respect it.
- **Manifestation:** Calling the growth of plants. Speaking with the spirits of beasts. Mending wounds through living moss and bark. Hiding paths from outsiders. Calling fog or wind from the forest.
- **Visual signature:** **Green-and-gold light.** Curling vines, bark patterns growing across skin, leaves spiraling in still air.
- **Practitioners:** Elven rangers, Council mages, hearthkeepers. Found in the Old Forest. Effectively unknown outside it.
- **Why outsiders confuse it for something else:** Because the elves rarely show it to humans, and when they do, it looks different from faith magic, humans sometimes mistake it for pagan or Old God power. **It is not.** It is the same Creator, honored through his work.

### Creation Magic (Dwarven Tradition)

- **Worship:** The dwarves worship the Creator **by creating.** Their belief: the Creator made the world, and the highest form of devotion is to **continue his work** — to shape stone, smith iron, raise mountains of cunning craft, build what has never been built before. *"Do as he did."*
- **Manifestation:** **Rune-craft.** Objects imbued with intent, persistence, function. Stone that holds. Iron that does not break. Golems that walk. Forges that never go cold. Gates that know friend from foe.
- **Visual signature:** **Glowing runes etched into worked metal and stone**, warm amber light deep in the work. Not flashy — *enduring.*
- **Practitioners:** Dwarven smiths, runesmiths, golem-wrights, masons. Almost all dwarves have *some* of this gift; their entire culture is shaped by it.
- **Where they went:** **The Hollowing.** Most dwarves fled north in 1000 BC after they cracked Noctheris's prison. Where they went, no human knows.
- **The exception:** **Durgan Cinderforge stayed.** (See Part 5.)

## Part 4 — The Corrupted Path (Old God Magic)

### How Noctheris Grants Power

Noctheris cannot create magic. She can only **steal and twist what was meant for the Creator.** Her power works through three steps:

1. **A weakness.** The receiver must have a real, personal weakness — grief, vanity, despair, lust, ambition, fear, a wound that never healed. Noctheris cannot create this weakness. She finds what is already there.
2. **A receptiveness.** The receiver must be **open to evil** — willing, on some level, to be helped by something dark. Often they do not know this about themselves. Noctheris does.
3. **A price already paid.** Power is **not free.** Someone must have given something up. Usually a child. Usually long before the power is granted.

### The Price of the Mothers

The clearest example: **the Mothers give up their unborn children.** They do not know, at the time, that they are striking a bargain. They are women who, at a moment of weakness — fear of motherhood, ambition, a desire to escape a husband, a desire to be free — make a prayer or a wish or a vow to Noctheris, sometimes without naming her. *They give the child away.*

**The child then receives the gift in their stead.** The mother is freed from the burden of the child. The child is born — or is born of the bargain — and grows up *carrying* the price already paid. They become Noctheris's. They can wield her power because the price was already paid before they were aware of themselves.

> **Ro'ak is the perfect example.** Elena, his mother, knowingly damned him before he was born. She used him as currency. She did not love him. **He became Noctheris's vessel because the price was paid in his blood before he drew breath.** He did not choose. He was given.

### How Noctheris Grants Power to Adults

Noctheris can also grant power to **adults** if she sees them as receptive and finds the weakness to exploit:

- **Seraphiel Moonwhisper** — vanity. She wanted to be more important than the Council. Noctheris told her she was a queen. She believed it. The price: her own soul, drawn out slowly, replaced with vine and thorn.
- **Ser Kael Sunstrike** — broken in captivity, tortured, despairing. Noctheris offered him the only relief he could imagine: power, and the cessation of his shame. He took it. The price: his holy fire turned violet. He knows what he was.
- **Durgan Cinderforge** — curiosity, two and a half millennia ago. He wanted to *learn.* Noctheris offered him the prison itself as a study. The price: extended life as her caretaker, and his creation-gift bent slowly to her service. (See Part 5.)

### What Corrupted Magic Looks Like

- **Visual signature:** **Violet flame, violet-green miasma, thorned vines, blackened runes, sickly light.** Where Creator magic is warm, this is *cold-bright.*
- **It can mirror Creator magic.** Kael's corrupted holy fire is a *dark mirror* of Van's. Seraphiel's nature magic is a dark mirror of the elves'. Durgan's rune-craft is a dark mirror of the dwarves'. This is the deepest horror of the corrupted path — **it looks almost the same.**
- **It costs the bearer.** Slowly, one piece of the soul at a time. By the time they realize what they have become, it is usually too late.

### The Five Old Gods

Noctheris is one of **Five Old Gods** sealed in the cracked prison. The others (Visstheri, Aqueloth, Drakhalion, Morhaem) operate by the same principle — they cannot make power, only twist what the Creator has given. They each have their own preferred weaknesses to exploit. **They have been listening to Noctheris's success for two centuries. They are taking notes.** *(Sequel material.)*

## Part 5 — The Curious Case of Durgan Cinderforge

Durgan deserves his own section because he is the **cleanest example of how Creator-gift becomes Noctheris-tool** without the bearer noticing until it is too late.

- **1000 BC, the Hollowing.** The dwarves of Halig, in the deepest mountain delves, broke into Noctheris's prison by accident. They saw enough to know what they had done. Their wisest counseled flight. The dwarves fled north.
- **Durgan stayed.** Not out of evil. Out of **fascination.** He was a master smith, a creator of cunning works, devout in the dwarven way — *do as he did.* He looked at the cracked prison and saw a thing of master-craft. He wanted to **understand it.** He believed he was honoring the Creator by studying the greatest puzzle ever made.
- **Noctheris noticed.** She saw the weakness — *curiosity, pride in his own craft, the desire to be the one dwarf who understood.* She did not strike. She **invited.**
- **Over centuries, she gave him gifts.** Extended life, so he could keep studying. Knowledge of older rune-forms. Materials he could not have found. Each gift seemed in keeping with his Creator-faith. Each gift bound him a little tighter.
- **By the time Ra'gor's clan came**, Durgan was already hers. He had been her caretaker for over a thousand years. The Black Spires were *his* design, raised on her foundation. The forges that fed Ro'ak's host were his. The Cinderforge Gate is his.
- **He still believes he is honoring the Creator.** *He is not.* He has not been for centuries. **But he has not noticed.** That is the deepest mark of the corrupted path — they do not know they have left it.

This is also why Durgan is **warm, friendly, treats fights as puzzles.** He has not lost his personality. He has only lost his alignment. *He is still the dwarf who wanted to understand.* He is just understanding for the wrong patron now.

## Part 6 — Technology Level

**Late medieval / sword-and-sorcery.** The Greenfields are roughly 13th–14th century England — water mills, stone bridges, plate armor for the wealthy, leather and gambeson for everyone else. Iron tools, woolen cloth, slate roofs.

- **No gunpowder.** No firearms.
- **Some clockwork, dwarven origin** — but extremely rare, mostly seen only at the Black Spires (Durgan's work).
- **Sail-ships.** Single-masted longships in Dreadvik (Scandinavian style). Larger merchant cogs along the Greenfields coast.
- **The wheel, the plow, the loom, the forge** — all present, standard.

## Part 7 — Religion & Belief

### Major Faiths

- **The Creator** — the true faith. Worshipped openly by humans (chapels of the Sun, hymns, sacrament), by elves (through their reverence for nature), by the dwarves who remember (through their creation craft).
- **The Old Gods (Five)** — heresy. Cultists worship them in secret. The Mothers serve Noctheris specifically. Ro'ak's host is built on this worship in the east.

### Religious Institutions

- **The Cathedral of the Sun, Crownhaven** — was the seat of the human Creator-faith. Fallen with the city.
- **Chapels of the Creator** — every Greenfields village has one. Hartwick has a great one. Fernfield's burned.
- **Elven Hearth-Circles** — open gathering places where the elves worship through communion with land and sky.
- **Dwarven Forge-Halls** — gone with the dwarves, except for what Durgan built into the Black Spires (now corrupted).
- **Mother Shrines** — hidden in cellars, warehouses, standing stones. Tended by the Mothers in secret.

### Heresies

- **Worship of any of the Five Old Gods** — punishable by death across all Creator-faithful regions.
- **Necromancy / raising the dead** — heresy and impossibility through Creator magic; only the Old Gods touch the dead, and they do it wrong.

### Are the Gods Real & Active?

- **The Creator: real, present, watching, acting through what already exists.** Never speaks directly. Always answers the truly faithful, in his own time, in his own way.
- **The Old Gods: real, imprisoned, reaching.** Noctheris is the most successful at reaching out. The other four are still mostly bound.

## Part 8 — Death & Afterlife

- **The faithful go to the Creator at death.** Quietly. There is no elaborate doctrine; it is simply known.
- **The unfaithful** — uncertain. Doctrine varies. Most believe they simply *end.* Some believe they linger.
- **Those given to Noctheris (Mothers' children, vessels)** — their souls do not go to the Creator. They are **taken** to the prison with her, to feed her. **This is what makes the Mothers' work damnation, not just death.**
- **No resurrection is possible through Creator magic.** Period.
- **Necromancy / raising the dead is the work of the Old Gods**, and what is raised is **not the person who died.** It is something wearing their shape that Noctheris has dragged back partly. **The Mothers themselves wield no magic** — they are ordinary women who have given their children to Noctheris, nothing more. When the dead walk near a Mother shrine, it is **Noctheris reaching through her vessels** (Ro'ak, Seraphiel, the chosen) and using the Mothers' offered grief as the price. The Mother provides the cost; the goddess does the work. **The party will encounter at least one of the raised.**

## Part 9 — Languages

- **Common (Aelden)** — the human tongue of the Greenfields, the Demesne, Dreadvik. Old-English-flavor.
- **Sindari** — the elven tongue, spoken only in the Old Forest. Most elves also speak Aelden when dealing with humans.
- **Khazdûl** — the dwarven tongue, almost extinct. Durgan speaks it; no one else who lives does.
- **The Whisper-Tongue** — what the Mothers and the lieutenants use among themselves. It is not a real language. It is **Noctheris's voice borrowed.** Hearing it spoken aloud is a sign of corruption nearby.

## Part 10 — Economy, Government, Law (Brief)

These matter less for *Fall of Ro'ak* than for sequels, but worth setting down:

- **Currency:** Crowns (gold, silver, copper) — Crownhaven-minted. With Crownhaven fallen, the system is fragile. Hartwick is still accepting them; Dreadvik never used them.
- **Major trade goods:** Greenfields grain and wool, Demesne wool and ore (formerly), Old Forest herbs (rare), Dreadvik fish and amber.
- **Government:** Was the Valerian crown across the Greenfields and Demesne. With Crownhaven fallen, government is **collapsing into local rule** — reeves, garrison captains, village elders. Dreadvik was always tribal.
- **Justice:** Village-level. Reeves and elders judge. Heresy is the one capital crime everyone agrees on.

## Part 11 — Game Systems

*Fall of Ro'ak* is a **hybrid auto-battler** drawing from three genres at once:

- **Story-progression auto-battler** — fixed party, narrative-driven, fights advance the journey east through major story beats.
- **Squad-tactics auto-battler** — loadout, positioning, and party configuration decisions before each fight.
- **Idle / incremental** — the **Region Rebuilding System** runs in the background, generating resources, raising buildings, and growing population while the player focuses on the road east.

### Party Composition

- **The four heroes — Runlan, Calypsa, Vallen, and Van — are locked for the entire game.** They are the player's party from Spring 500 AC to Winter 500 AC, start to finish.
- **Followers** can be added to the party two ways:
  - **Recruited along the road east** as the party travels and meets willing warriors.
  - **Trained at the villages.** Once a village is rebuilt to a sufficient level, the player can train villagers for combat and pull them into the follower roster.
- Followers join battles and fight on their own AI logic, alongside the heroes.
- **Followers can die permanently in combat.** The heroes cannot — their deaths are prevented by narrative necessity. They must survive for the story to conclude.

### Player Control in Combat

*Fall of Ro'ak* is a **gear-and-loadout game**, not a real-time tactics game.

- **Before battle**, the player:
  - Equips gear (weapons, armor, accessories) on each hero and follower
  - Stocks consumables (potions, food, faith-relics, etc.)
  - Sets party positioning on the battle grid
  - Configures AI behavior toggles — including **how the AI should treat a downed ally** (e.g. whether and when to spend a revive potion on a downed hero or follower)
- **Once battle begins**, the player **watches.** Combat plays out automatically:
  - Spells and abilities fire based on each character's AI logic
  - Heroes target, position, and react on their own
  - The player **does not** manually activate abilities or issue orders during the fight
- The skill ceiling is in the **preparation**, not the swing.

### Replayable Fights

- **Fights are not strictly one-shot scripted beats.** Once a fight has been cleared, the player can **return to it and re-engage** to farm materials, gear, and consumables.
- This supports the gear-and-loadout core loop — players who want better drops can grind earlier encounters before moving east.
- Story-critical fights still occur in narrative order, but the **map remains open** to revisit previously cleared encounters at the player's discretion.

### Progression

Heroes **do not earn XP or level up traditionally.** Power scales through four channels:

1. **Gear** — stronger weapons, armor, and accessories as the journey progresses east. Loot drops from combat (including farmed re-runs of earlier fights), found in dungeons/ruins, traded in town.
2. **Consumables** — more powerful potions, faith-relics, and one-shot items unlock as the game advances.
3. **Region Rebuilding** — every town or village restored to working order grants **passive stat bonuses** to the party as long as it stands. A rebuilt chapel buffs Van's holy magic. A rebuilt smithy improves weapon quality. A rebuilt herb garden buffs Calypsa's healing. Etc.
4. **Recruited NPCs** — a larger and more capable follower roster makes encounters progressively easier. **The party effectively gets bigger as you go.**

### Death & Failure Handling

- **A hero is downed when their HP reaches 1**, not zero. **Downed heroes remain on the battlefield** — they do not leave the fight.
- Downed heroes can be **revived mid-combat** by a revive potion. The decision of *whether and when* to use a revive potion is governed by the **pre-battle AI toggles** the player configured. (Want the AI to burn potions aggressively to keep heroes up? Set it that way. Want the AI to hoard potions and let the downed hero ride it out? Set it that way.)
- **If all four heroes are downed simultaneously, the party flees.** Combat ends immediately. The party retreats to the nearest camp and must **fully heal** before re-attempting the encounter.
- **Followers do not share the heroes' narrative protection.** A follower whose HP reaches 0 **dies permanently.** Their gear is recovered; they are not.
- **There is no game-over state.** The story always continues. The party can always retry. **The cost of failure is in who you lose along the way — not in starting over.**

### The Region Rebuilding System

What was once called "village building" expands by mid-game into a true **regional restoration project.** As the party moves east, they unlock more places to rebuild:

- **Fernfield** — unlocked at the opening. The Skye family's home village. First rebuild project.
- **Hartwick** — unlocked early. Less rebuilding (it wasn't destroyed), more **expansion and reinforcement** as it fills with refugees.
- **Crownhaven** — unlocked when the party arrives at the fallen capital. **The largest, longest, most ambitious rebuild** of the campaign. This is the project that defines the back half of the game.
- **(Possibly others as the campaign demands.)**

**By the end of the game, these regions are better than they were before Ro'ak's destruction.** The cost was terrible. The result is a stronger Eadenreach. *That is the player's true victory — not just killing Ro'ak, but rebuilding what he tried to erase.*

#### The Caravan Route

The Region Rebuilding System is connected to the journey by a **caravan route** the player establishes as they travel:

- As the party moves east, they **open caravan stops** along the Crown Road.
- **Villagers send basic resources to the party** — food, healing herbs, common consumables — via the caravan route.
- **The party sends back special items** found in combat (rare materials, relics, monster components) that the villages need for upgrades.
- The caravan route is **the connective tissue** between the idle/incremental side (passive village growth) and the active side (the journey).
- **The caravan route is narratively safe.** There are **no ambushes, no lost caravans, and no risk-reward system** layered on top of it. The player can be confident their materials always make it to their destination, both ways. The narrative simply guarantees safe passage along the road behind the party.

#### The Elder Couple

The player interacts with the Region Rebuilding System through a **village elder couple** — the management interface character(s) for the whole rebuild effort. They:

- Coordinate the rebuilds across all villages and towns the player unlocks
- Take requests from the player for what to prioritize
- Report on construction progress, population growth, and resource needs
- Send the player updates and small story moments as the regions grow

### The Ending

The game ends with Ro'ak defeated and Eadenreach saved. The party's fates:

- **Runlan and Calypsa** return to **Fernfield** (rebuilt, restored, alive again) to live out the rest of their lives in peace in their home village. *They have done enough.*
- **Vallen** becomes **King of Crownhaven**, restored. He is the new ruler of the rebuilt human kingdom, founding a new dynasty in the place where Valerian fell.
- **Van** becomes **the Right Hand of the King** — Vallen's most trusted advisor, paladin, and defender. He stands where his faith always meant for him to stand: at the side of a just king, in the rebuilt heart of the realm.

*This is the single ending of the game.* No branching paths, no romance, no alternate finales. **One story, one outcome, well told.**
