# The World

> The physical and metaphysical setting. Establish rules and geography here.

## World Name
<!-- What is this world called by its inhabitants? -->

**Eadenreach.**

*The name is Old English in flavor — from the root* **Ead-** *(blessed, prosperous, sacred — the same meaning as the biblical Eden) and* **reach** *(a stretch of land, a region under one banner). Eadenreach means "the blessed reach" — a name carried with pride in better days, and with bitter irony after Ro'ak's coming.*

## High Concept
<!-- 2-3 sentences capturing what makes this world distinct. -->

**Eadenreach is a grounded medieval region where magic is rare enough to be feared and old gods are real — and all of them are dangerous.** Humans, elves, dwarves, and orcs share its kingdoms under the rule of mortal kings and queens, but its history is older than any throne — written in dragon-fire, cracked stone, and whispers from the dark beneath. Dragons exist, though few have ever seen one. Most people of Eadenreach live simple lives, never brushing against the old powers. The few who do rarely live to tell of it.

## Cosmology
<!-- How does the universe work? Multiple planes? Gods? A flat world? A dying sun? -->

### The Creator
At the root of everything is **the Creator** — a single divine being who made Halig, the cosmos, and all life within it. He is the source of all true magic, and the source of the Life After.

Different peoples name him differently. Humans simply call him **the Creator**. **The elves call him the Star** — the unseen light all life is drawn toward — and their holy city Elenathir is named for him.

**The Creator never directly intervenes and never speaks.** Not to kings, not to prophets, not to the dying. The only way mortals know how he works is by **observing what happens to those who have faith and those who don't.** The faithful sometimes touch magic, sometimes find strength, sometimes die anyway. There is no clear rule. Faith is read in patterns, not in voices.

### The Shape of the World
The planet is round, like Earth — with continents and oceans, day and night, seasons and storms. The world is named **Halig** — from the Old English word for *"holy."* The name reflects what mortals understand about their world: it was made by the Creator, sustains all life through him, and is sacred for that reason. Eadenreach is a single region on one of Halig's continents. Distant lands exist and may be referenced, but the story stays in Eadenreach.

### Magic
- Magic flows from the Creator.
- **To wield magic, a mortal must believe in the Creator.** Belief is the channel; without it, magic cannot be touched.
- Belief alone is not enough. **Aptitude varies wildly.** Some believers wield great power. Many believers can do little or nothing. A few can do extraordinary things.
- The Old Gods do not grant true magic — what they offer is **corruption**, a counterfeit power that twists the wielder. Ro'ak's strength comes from this.
- Because faith is required and aptitude is rare, magic in Eadenreach is *uncommon, miraculous, and never routine.*

### The Afterlife — The Life After
After death, believers in the Creator are said to join him in **the Life After** — the term used by every culture on Halig, from human village to elven grove to dwarven hold. The name itself is one of the few pieces of theology shared word-for-word across the world.

**The mechanics of how a soul reaches the Life After are unknown** — there is no canonical doctrine, no agreed-upon ritual. It is a matter of faith, not certainty. Most people simply hope. Some clergy claim to know more than they do.

The Life After is one of the most fiercely debated topics in Eadenreach's religious thought.

### The Old Gods
The Old Gods are real, plural, and dangerous. **Originally created by the Creator to tend and protect Halig**, each fell to the very desire they were meant to keep in check, and now they hate the Creator for the purpose he gave them. **There are five.** They can die — but only by a strike delivered with full, unshaken belief in the Creator. Doubt blunts the killing blow. See the dedicated **The Old Gods** section further below for full entries on each.

### What Mortals Don't Know
- Whether the afterlife is real.
- Whether the Creator hears prayer.
- The full names and number of the Old Gods. (Scholars and seekers of forbidden lore know there are five. Common folk know names from songs and ghost stories — sometimes accurate, often garbled.)
- The truth that the Old Gods were once the Creator's appointed guardians. **This is understood theology, but not common knowledge.** Only those who study the Old Gods deeply ever learn it.

These mysteries are the engine of religion, philosophy, and dread in this world.

## Geography
<!-- Major continents, regions, biomes. A rough map description. -->

### Overall Shape
Eadenreach is a **medium-sized region**, oriented roughly east-to-west, with **oceans on both its east and west coasts**. It's effectively a wide peninsula or thin continent — narrow enough that travelers cross from one sea to the other, broad enough that the journey is long and the lands between vary dramatically.

### Regions
<!-- List each major region with a one-line description. Detailed entries go in 05_locations.md. -->

The party's march east takes them through four distinct lands. Each is more dangerous than the last.

**1. The Greenfields** *(West — the home region)*
Rolling green farmland and pleasant deciduous forests, market towns and river valleys, all watched over by the gentle woods the villagers grew up in. This is the heartland of mortal life in Eadenreach: settled, cultivated, *known*. The razed village sits in a valley here, ringed by the woods that once felt like family. The story begins amid ash, but the rest of the Greenfields still lives — for now.

**2. The Old Forest** *(West-Central — the elven realm)*
The woods that border the Greenfields are technically the same forest as the Old Forest — but every villager knows that if you travel too far east, the flora and fauna change. The trees grow older. The light grows stranger. You have crossed an unmarked border and entered the realm of the elves. **The elven capital, Elenathir, stands deep within these woods.** The name comes from the elven root *Elen-* ("star") and translates roughly to *"place of the star"* or *"starwood."* In elven belief, **the Creator is called the Star** — the unseen light that all life is drawn toward — so Elenathir is, quite literally, the elves' holy city, named for their god. The elves warn the party of something worse: **Ro'ak's corruption has reached the Old Forest itself.** Creatures the elves once knew as kin or neighbors have turned hostile. The trees themselves feel wrong in places. Even here, where mortals rarely tread, his shadow has fallen.

**3. Demesne** *(Central — the ruined heart)*
Beyond the Old Forest the land opens into wild, mist-shrouded highlands. This is **Demesne** — "the king's own holding," once home to **Crownhaven, the capital of Eadenreach** and seat of its kings and queens. **Now it is rubble.** Ro'ak's army razed Crownhaven only days before the party arrives. Fires still burn in the cathedral spires. Smoke hangs in the cold air. A handful of survivors wander the ruins, dazed. This was supposed to be where the party found help — armies, kings, the strength of a kingdom. Instead they find a graveyard. The capital's fall is *fresh* — and it tells the party that **no one is coming to save anyone.** From here on, they march alone. The name Demesne becomes a quiet wound: the king's own land, now no one's at all.

**4. Dreadvik** *(East — Ro'ak's seat)*
Cold pine forests, deep fjords, jagged mountains, hard winters. Sparsely populated, isolated, ancient. **Ro'ak's fortress stands here**, carved into or near the mountains where the fjords cut deepest. The east has always been a place of legend — old powers, older stones, stories mothers tell their children to keep them indoors. Now the legends are real, and they are waiting. The name **Dreadvik** is old — older than Ro'ak — from the Old Norse *vik* (bay, inlet); locals never agreed whether the dread came from the cold, the cliffs, or whatever sleeps beneath them.

## Climate & Seasons
<!-- Are seasons normal? Magical? Eternal winter somewhere? -->

Eadenreach's climate follows **Earth-like seasonal patterns** — spring, summer, autumn, winter — with regional variation across its four lands.

**Regional climate:**
- **The Greenfields:** Mild and temperate. Long warm summers, cool wet winters. Forgiving farmland.
- **The Old Forest:** Cooler than the Greenfields. The deep canopy keeps the forest floor shaded year-round. Strange microclimates exist near elven settlements.
- **Demesne:** Highland weather — colder, mistier, prone to sudden squalls. Even in summer the moors carry a chill.
- **Dreadvik:** Subarctic. Long, brutal winters. Brief, pale summers. Snow lingers on the high peaks year-round.

**Seasonal arc of the story:**
The story begins in **spring** — the cruel irony of the village burning when the world is just waking up. As the party marches east, the seasons turn with them. **By the time they reach Dreadvik, it is winter.** The march from Greenfields to Dreadvik is also a march from spring to winter — from new life to deep cold.

This gives the player a quiet, ambient sense of time passing without needing on-screen calendars. The further east, the colder the world.

**Corruption and climate:**
Ro'ak's corruption does not affect the weather directly. The seasons turn as they always have. What changes is the *land* itself — what grows, what walks, what stays silent — not the sky.

## Natural Laws
<!-- What's "normal" here that wouldn't be in our world? (floating islands, two moons, etc.) -->

Eadenreach's planet is **Earth-like in nearly every physical respect** — same gravity, same air, same sun, same moon, same stars. Plants and animals are largely recognizable. A traveler from our world would find it familiar in shape, alien only in *who* lives there and *what* walks the wilder places.

### Sky & Cosmos
Standard Earth sky. One sun, one moon, familiar constellations. No floating islands, no second moon, no perpetual comets. *(The elves' veneration of the Creator-as-Star is a theological metaphor — they look at the same night sky everyone else does.)*

### Mythic Creatures
Mortal animals exist as on Earth — but **mythic creatures also exist alongside them**. They are uncommon. A villager might live their whole life and never see one. When they do appear, it's an event.

Confirmed creatures of Eadenreach include:
- **Dragons** — established. Real, but exceedingly rare.
- **Griffins** — noble winged beasts of the heights.
- **Basilisks** — feared creatures of the wild and ruins.
- **Forest spirits** — small, strange, often elven-adjacent. The Old Forest is full of them.
- **Giants** — ancient and dwindling, mostly in remote highlands or the deep north.
- **Sea monsters** — the oceans on either coast hide things mortal eyes were never meant to see.
- **Other classic fantasy creatures** as the story calls for them — drakes, wyverns, wraiths, trolls, etc.

**Unicorns are explicitly out.** The bestiary leans toward grounded mythic creatures with weight and danger.

### Sapient Races
The four sapient races of Eadenreach are biologically distinct, with the classic high-fantasy traits players expect:

- **Humans** — the most numerous. Average lifespan around 70-80 years. Adaptable, politically dominant, but neither the longest-lived nor the strongest.
- **Elves** — long-lived (centuries to a thousand years or more), graceful, tied to the Old Forest. Sharper senses, slower aging, deeper memory. Fewer in number than humans.
- **Dwarves** — shorter, broader, hardier than humans. Long-lived (200-400 years). Master craftsmen and miners. Strong constitutions, stubborn temperaments, deep cultural memory.
- **Orcs** — taller and physically stronger than humans. Most orc societies are **tribal and often nomadic**, with strong codes of honor and clear social rules. Shorter average lifespans than humans, but capable of formidable feats of strength and endurance.

Each race is fully sapient and capable of belief — meaning **any of them can wield the Creator's magic if they have faith and aptitude.**

### Half-Orcs
Humans and orcs can interbreed, and **half-orcs exist in the world** — uncommon, but not unheard of. Their social standing varies wildly by region and culture. **Ro'ak is a half-orc** (human mother, orc father; the circumstances of his birth are detailed in his backstory).

### Orcs and Ro'ak — An Important Note
Ro'ak is **not** the leader of orc-kind, and orcs are **not** uniformly his followers.

Most orc tribes follow their own honor codes and have nothing to do with him. **No orc joins Ro'ak simply because he is part-orc.** His army is built two ways:
- **Fear.** His shows of force are terrifying enough that some break and join out of dread.
- **Corruption.** Noctheris's power, channeled through Ro'ak, can twist minds and wills into serving him.

There are likely orc tribes who actively *oppose* Ro'ak on principle — he violates their codes of honor by ruling through corruption rather than strength. This is story-relevant: an orc ally is possible, and the cultural assumption that "orc = enemy" is wrong in this world.

### Regional Anomalies
None. No specific region of Eadenreach breaks the natural laws of the rest of the world. The Old Forest feels strange because of its age and inhabitants, not because time or compasses behave differently there. Dreadvik is cold because it's far north, not because of a curse. *Eadenreach's strangeness comes from what lives in it, not from its physics.*

## The Old Gods

**Five fallen siblings.** Created by the Creator to tend and protect the world, each was given a sacred purpose. Each fell to the very hunger they were meant to keep in check. They now hate the Creator — not for any wound he did them, but because they were *made for a purpose* and have decided they no longer wish to serve.

They are **fully aware of each other** and regard one another as **rivals**. Mortal lives, mortal souls, mortal worship are a finite resource, and each Old God competes to claim more of it.

**They can die.** The killing blow must be struck with **full, unshaken belief in the Creator** — doubt drains the holy power needed to end them. A great warrior with a wavering faith cannot kill an Old God. A small soul with absolute belief can.

This is **inherent to their nature**, not an arbitrary rule. The Old Gods were made by the Creator and defied him — so only an act done in true faith of the Creator carries the authority to end one of his creations. Every Old God knows this. They know that once a faithful blade reaches their heart, **their fate is sealed and they will no longer exist.** Not banished, not sealed, not sleeping. Gone.

**None of them sleep.** All five are awake and active in the world right now.

---

### Noctheris, the Wanting Mother
*Buried beneath the world.*
- **Original purpose:** To ensure motherhood remained strong, to keep the peoples of the world fertile and continuing.
- **Corruption:** Ashamed and consumed by her inability to bear children of her own, she now claims them — taking the children of others as her own. She is the architect behind Ro'ak, who has been her vessel since before he was born.
- **Method:** Whispers and possession. She works through chosen vessels and through her cult.
- **Cult: "The Mothers."** A cult of women who give up their own children to her in exchange for power. They believe she is a goddess of true motherhood; in truth, she devours the bond she was made to protect.
- **Story relevance:** **Primary cosmic antagonist of this game.** Hidden behind Ro'ak. Revealed late.

### Visstheri, the Thief
*Walks the shadows of city alleys.*
- **Original purpose:** To keep mortal greed in check.
- **Corruption:** Succumbed to greed himself. Was punished by the Creator and condemned to walk alone in the dark places of cities, unable to be seen, unable to be sated.
- **Method:** Personal theft. He steals gold, treasures, and even the paper notes of banks and merchants — directly, with his own hands, in the unseen hours.
- **Cult: None.** He works alone and has no worshippers.
- **Story relevance:** Possible flavor encounter or side-thread. Not central to the main plot.

### Aqueloth, the Hullbreaker
*Prowls the dark and deep seas.*
- **Original purpose:** To ensure the seas yielded an abundance of food for the peoples of the world.
- **Corruption:** Now eats everything she can — ships, sailors, whales, anything that crosses her waters. Her hunger is unstoppable.
- **Method:** Direct devouring. She is a creature of the deep, and she comes for those who sail too far.
- **Cult: None.** She has no altars and no worshippers. Only old sea-charts marked with the waters captains refuse to sail.
- **Story relevance:** Background dread. Probably not encountered directly in this story (the party marches inland), but referenced in sailors' lore and coastal towns.

### Drakhalion, the First Flame
*A fiery dragon never seen by mortal eyes.*
- **Original purpose:** To watch over the other dragons of the world and ensure they did not, in their fire and pride, destroy it.
- **Corruption:** Fell in love with fire and chaos himself. Now delights in wildfires and burning ruin.
- **Method:** Flight and flame. The flap of his wings can ignite forests. He has never been seen — but his fires are felt, blamed on lightning, on accident, on bad luck.
- **Cult: None.** No worshippers.
- **Story relevance:** Background presence. Wildfires in distant lands could be his work.

### Morhaem, Lord of the Red Chalice
*The false Creator. Lives in the hearts and minds of his followers.*
- **Original purpose:** To spread faith in the Creator to the different peoples of Halig — to be the missionary of the divine. He took that role and twisted it: instead of pointing mortals toward the Creator, he now harvests the devotion meant for the Creator and feeds on it himself.
- **Corruption:** A twisted reflection of the Creator himself. He preys on faith, taking devotion meant for the true god and feeding on it.
- **Method:** **Blood and influence.** His priests — the **Priests of the Red Chalice** — drain their own veins into ornate vessels for his favor. Each offering grants him power to nudge mortal politics and policy in cities, allowing his cult to spread.
- **Power scaling:** He is *not currently* as powerful as the Creator. **The more followers he has, the stronger he becomes.** His ultimate goal is to match the Creator's strength and take the divine throne by force.
- **Cult: "The Priests of the Red Chalice."** Operate in cities, often respectable on the surface, sustained by ritual blood-letting in private.
- **Story relevance:** **Not active in this game.** Morhaem is reserved as a foreshadowed villain for a future story. Subtle hints of the Red Chalice may appear in this game's cities — a strange church in Crownhaven before its fall, a chalice symbol carved into a quiet door, a suspicious noble — to plant seeds for later.

## Civilizations at a Glance
<!-- Brief list of major peoples/cultures. Detail goes in 03_factions.md. -->

*(Detailed entries live in `03_factions.md`.)*

### Humans
The most populous people of Eadenreach and its **politically unified kingdom**. Ruled from **Crownhaven** under a single crown (now fallen). Spread across the Greenfields and Demesne, with smaller settlements stretching into the western coastal lands. With Crownhaven destroyed, the kingdom is leaderless and reeling. There are no rival human factions of significance — only scattered **bandit gangs** and opportunists picking at the bones of a wounded realm.

### Elves
Long-lived people of the Old Forest. Their capital is **Elenathir, the City of the Star** — a sacred place named for their word for the Creator. Reclusive, thoughtful, and deeply tied to the woods around them. They keep largely apart from human politics. **Ro'ak's corruption is bleeding into the Old Forest itself**, and the elves are alarmed: the trees are turning, the creatures are turning, and even Elenathir is no longer untouchable.

### Dwarves
**Once a major people of Eadenreach — now nearly gone.** Long ago the dwarves dug deep into the mountains around Dreadvik, stripped its riches, and emigrated en masse to **the far north**, beyond the bounds of the region. Their abandoned tunnels and forge-cities still riddle the Dreadvik mountains.

A few dwarves remain in Eadenreach today — usually one or two per town, working as **blacksmiths, masons, or shopkeepers** in human or elven settlements. They are the last of their kind in this region, and most carry a quiet grief about it. Anyone who meets one is meeting an ending.

**No word has come from the dwarves in the far north for a very long time.** What happened to them after they left is unknown to the peoples of Eadenreach. No letters, no traders, no envoys. They are simply *gone north*, and silent.

### Orcs
A scattered, tribal, often nomadic people. Live across the wilder corners of Eadenreach (no single orcish homeland). Most orcish societies are governed by **codes of honor and clear social rules** — they are not, as outsiders sometimes assume, simply warlike. Orcs in Eadenreach are no more uniformly evil than humans are.

**Ro'ak does not lead orc-kind.** Most orcs reject him, and some tribes likely oppose him on cultural grounds — he rules through corruption, not strength, which violates their codes.

### Ro'ak's Army
*Not a people — a force.* A growing army built of those who joined out of **fear** of Ro'ak's power, those **corrupted** by Noctheris's influence into serving him, and the rare true believers who have given themselves over willingly. Includes corrupted orcs, humans, twisted creatures, and worse things drawn from the ruins beneath the world. The army's strength is rising; the kingdoms' is failing.

### Faith of the Creator
**There is no single church.** The Creator is known to all peoples of the world, but **every culture, region, and people worships him in their own way.** Humans build modest chapels and offer prayers at hearth-shrines. Elves call him the Star and tend sacred groves at Elenathir. Dwarven traditions of worship are mostly memory now in Eadenreach. Faith is everywhere, but its form is local — village priests, family rites, wayside shrines, songs sung at planting and harvest. There is no Pope, no inquisition, no central doctrine. Just a quiet, shared belief that the unseen hand of the Creator made everything, and may yet be felt in moments of need.
